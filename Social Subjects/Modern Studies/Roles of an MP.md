# In Parliament
## Scrutinise the Government
One of the roles of MP's is to hold the government to account.
	$\to$ Checking on their success.

### PMQ's (Prime Ministers Questions)
PMQ's take place every **Wednesday**. During this, the PM is asked by other members of the house about the Governments performance and how the Government will respond to certain events. **PMQ's** get the most **media attention**.
**However**, many argue that PMQ's are just for the media coverage. While the PM has not seen the questions, he will likely be briefed on what he is likely to be asked. During PMQ's the PM's **own party** often **ask questions**, giving the party a chance to tell the public how great they are doing. During PMQ's the leader of the opposition takes lots of time arguing with the PM, and trying to make him seem incompetent.
 For example,
- Kemi Badenoch(Leader of the Opposition) and other members of the house(SNP MP) questioned Keir Starmer on his **judgement** over the **appointment of Peter Mandelson as British ambassador to the USA**.
- Conservative MP challenging the PM and the Government about the use of hotels in small towns in England to house asylum seekers - "Bell Hotel" SA of a local girl from an asylum seeker

### Department Question Time
MP's also get to question Government ministers face to face about the work of their departments. On a rota, each minister has to answer questions for about 30-60 minutes.

### Written questions:
This form of asking the government questions is more common, but the most under-reported, letters are boring.
MP's can write questions to a government department and expect an answer within 7 days. This is usually what MP's use to scrutinise the government and/or represent their constituents.

#### Example:
Ian Murray asked the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs about the environmental impact of 5G.

## Debating and Voting
One role that MP's have is **holding debates and voting** in Parliament.
**Debating** allows MP's to act as the '**voice of the people**' and raise issues about their constituencies.

**Debates** are important as they allow MP's to scrutinise the Government by asking questions and putting their alternative views across.
However, MP's are not guaranteed to be heard in debates and must try to get the attention of the Speaker by standing up or waving papers - this is called "bobbing".

**Voting** allows MP's to directly transfer the views of their Constituents into law. But MP's are influenced by many different people and groups, so the decision that they make isn't often straight forward. MP's will often vote the way their party wishes.

#### Example:
Ian Murray voted to take control of parliament's timetable in September 2019 in an attempt to sort out the Brexit crisis.

## Serving on a Committee 
Committee are small groups of MP's who meet on regular basis to  **scrutinise** the work of the government. Most MP's are members of at least one committee.
Commitees allow MP's to **research and have influence on a specific area or issue** on which the government makes decisions. They conduct **inquiries**, gather **written evidence and interview experts**. The can also call on the government to give evidence.

# In their constituency
An MP's job is to **represent their constituents** - Since in the UK we live in a **representative democracy**. This means that MP's are supposed to act as 'the voice' of the people they represent and make decisions that benefit them best on their behalf - including making new laws.
MP's are also expected to 'hold the government to account'. This means that they have opportunities to question the government about their decisions. 
MP's split their work in two - they work in their **constituencys on a Friday - Sunday** and in **Parliament (in London) Monday to Thursday**.

In their Constituency, MP's:
### Attend Meetings:
MP's often meet with a wide variety of people in their local constituencies. Councillors, local business and local pressure groups all want to meet with an MP in the hope their issues can be raised by the MP in parliament.

### Visits and social events:
An MP is regarded as a high profile guest by many organisations. MP's will attend events such as the opening of a new building, or an awards ceremony. MP's may also visit local schools to talk with pupils. All of this allow MP's to gather views of their constituents.

### Local media:
In order to keep a good rapport with their constituents, MP's often appear in local newspapers or on local TV stations. This is an effective way to engage with the local voting base en-mass.

### Surgeries:
Usually scheduled once a week MP's often hold a 'surgery' in their office, where local people can come along to discuss any matters that concern them, locally or nationally.
